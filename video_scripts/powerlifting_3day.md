## Intro (~60 seconds)

If your goal is to squat, bench, and deadlift heavier, and you want a programme that actually peaks you toward a test week rather than just giving you random workouts, this one is for you.

The Traditional Powerlifting programme in Tempered Strength is an eight-week peaking block built around the big three. Monday is squat focus, Wednesday is bench focus, Friday is deadlift focus. About sixty minutes per session, three days a week.

It is free, marked intermediate, and designed for one outcome: week eight is testing week, where you attempt new one-rep maxes on squat, bench, and deadlift.

I will walk you through how the eight weeks build, what each day looks like, and whether this fits where you are right now.

## Main body (~5–6 minutes)

### The big picture

This is a classic peaking structure. Volume is higher early, intensity climbs through the block, and accessories taper as you approach testing week.

Weeks one to three: main lifts at eight reps, accessories at eight to ten reps, intensity around seven.

Weeks four to six: main lifts drop to five reps, accessories at six to eight, intensity at eight.

Weeks seven and the build into week eight: main lifts at two to three reps, accessories at five to six, intensity at nine.

Week eight is testing week. One rep max attempts on squat Monday, bench Wednesday, deadlift Friday. The programme strips back to what matters: warm up properly, build through progressive singles, then attempt your max.

### Squat day (Monday)

Squat-specific warmup: hip mobility, leg swings, glute bridges, bodyweight squats.

Competition squat is the main event. Sets increase from four early in the block to five in the middle weeks. Romanian deadlifts for posterior chain support, with rep ranges that taper as the programme peaks. Leg press for quad hypertrophy to help you out of the hole. Planks for bracing practice.

On testing day, you take four to six progressively heavier warm-up sets, then attempt your new max. Root your feet, create intra-abdominal pressure, controlled descent, explosive ascent.

### Bench day (Wednesday)

Bench-specific warmup with pec stretches, band pull-aparts, face pulls, scapular push-ups, and empty-bar bench pressing.

Competition bench press as the main lift. Overhead press for shoulder strength that supports a stable bench. Seated cable rows because a bigger back gives you a bigger shelf to press from. Face pulls to close the session.

Face pulls are non-negotiable here. Eight weeks of heavy pressing without rear delt and rotator cuff work is how benchers end up injured. The programme builds this in deliberately.

### Deadlift day (Friday)

Deadlift-specific warmup with hinge mechanics, good mornings, bird dogs, and empty-bar Romanian deadlifts.

Competition deadlift: three working sets through the block, tapering reps as intensity climbs. Bulgarian split squats for unilateral leg strength and hip stability. Pull-ups for lat strength that keeps the bar close during heavy pulls, with swap options available. Hanging leg raises for core work that does not add spinal flexion load after a heavy pulling day.

### What to expect

You should squat, bench, and deadlift heavier than when you started. Eight weeks of structured peaking with a tighter, more powerful frame to show for it. This is not a bodybuilding programme. You will not get dedicated arm days or high-volume isolation work. You will get stronger on the lifts that matter in powerlifting.

### Who is this for?

Good fit if you know the basic squat, bench, and deadlift patterns, you can train three days a week, and you want to test your maxes at the end of a block. Less ideal if you are brand new to barbell training, want a physique-focused split, or cannot commit to eight weeks of progressive loading.

You need a gym with a squat rack, bench, barbell, and standard accessories. Intermediate means you should be comfortable under a barbell before starting.
