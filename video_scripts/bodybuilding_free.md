## Main body (~5–6 minutes)

### The big picture

The whole programme is built around one idea: hit each muscle group with enough volume to grow, recover properly between sessions, and gradually increase the challenge over four weeks.

You are not randomising exercises every time you walk in. You are following a plan. That matters, because muscle growth responds best to consistent stimulus over time, not just one brutal session followed by a week of guessing.

The app categorises this under bodybuilding, with goals of bulking, hypertrophy, and getting stronger. If you want a programme that prioritises how you look and feel in the mirror, with strength as a natural by-product, you are in the right place.

### How the four weeks progress

Weeks one and two ease you in. Your main compound lifts sit in the ten to twelve rep range. Accessories sit around twelve to fifteen reps. Intensity is rated seven out of ten.

Weeks three and four step things up. The rep ranges drop slightly: compounds move to eight to ten reps, accessories to ten to twelve. Intensity goes up to eight. On the final leg day of week four, that session peaks at nine out of ten.

What that means in practice is simple. You are not necessarily adding weight every single session. The programme tapers the reps so the same weight becomes harder work. That is progressive overload, and it is one of the reasons this block feels like a real training cycle rather than a random workout list.

There is also built-in variation across the weeks. Even weeks lean barbell-heavy. Odd weeks swap in dumbbells and machines where it makes sense. So you get the stability of a fixed split, but you are not doing the exact same session four times in a row.

### Day one: Chest and triceps (Monday)

Every upper body day starts with a proper warmup. Light cardio for three to five minutes, then mobility and activation: pec and lat stretches, arm circles, band pull-aparts or scapular wall slides, scapular push-ups, and some light overhead pressing to groove the bar path. It takes a few minutes and it is worth doing.

The main work opens with your primary chest press. On even weeks that is barbell bench press: four sets, controlled two-second descent, touch the lower chest, drive up without bouncing. On odd weeks it switches to dumbbell bench press, which lets you get a deeper stretch at the bottom. Same muscle, slightly different stimulus.

After that you hit incline dumbbell press for upper chest, then cable flyes for constant tension through the pecs. The app tells you not to lock out at the top on flyes, and that is deliberate. You want the muscle working the whole time.

Triceps get direct work too. Skull crushers one week, overhead tricep extensions the next. Three sets each, isolation focus, elbows controlled.

The session finishes with reverse flyes. Light weight, high reps, fifteen to twenty. This is shoulder health work, not ego lifting. After a full day of pressing, your front delts and pecs have done plenty. Reverse flyes balance that out and help keep your shoulders feeling good week after week.

### Day two: Back and biceps (Wednesday)

Same upper warmup, then into the pulling work.

Your main row alternates weekly. Barbell rows on even weeks for back thickness: torso parallel to the floor, pull with your elbows. Dumbbell rows on odd weeks for unilateral control, pulling to the hip to keep the lat engaged.

For vertical pulling, even weeks use pull-ups, six to ten reps. Odd weeks use lat pulldown at the same rep range as your other compounds. Pull-ups can be swapped in the app if you need to, which is useful if you are still building up to bodyweight reps.

Then seated cable rows for more mid-back volume, a squeeze at the peak contraction, three sets in that higher accessory rep range.

Biceps get isolated with dumbbell curls one week and preacher curls the next. In weeks three and four, the bicep exercise is marked as AMRAP on your final set, so you can push hard when the programme is at its most demanding.

The session closes with sit-ups: three sets of eight to twelve, controlled, no yanking on your neck. A bit of direct ab work without loading your spine after a heavy pulling day.

### Day three: Legs and shoulders (Friday)

Leg day uses a different warmup: light bike, hip flexor and pigeon stretches, leg swings, hip circles, glute bridges with a pause, and bodyweight squats focusing on depth and control.

The main leg compound alternates between barbell back squat and leg press. Four sets, same progressive rep scheme as the rest of the programme. Squat weeks emphasise sitting back and down, chest proud, at least parallel at the bottom. Leg press weeks keep your back glued to the seat and drive through the quads.

Romanian deadlifts come next every week. Three sets, hip hinge, deep hamstring stretch, glutes squeezed at the top. This is your posterior chain staple across the whole block.

Isolation alternates: leg extensions for quads on even weeks, leg curls for hamstrings on odd weeks. Both are swappable if your gym setup differs.

Shoulders come after the leg work. Barbell overhead press on even weeks, strict, no leg drive, core braced. Dumbbell shoulder press on odd weeks, full range, no momentum. Then lateral raises for side delt width, and face pulls to finish.

Face pulls matter. After overhead pressing, your rotator cuff has been under load. Face pulls with high reps and external rotation at the end help protect the shoulder long term. Same idea as the reverse flyes on chest day: this programme builds muscle, but it also tries not to wreck your joints in the process.

### What to expect from four weeks

The app describes the body changes honestly. You should look more developed across your chest, back, and legs. Four weeks is enough to feel pumps come on faster, dial in form on the main lifts, and add visible tightness if your nutrition is on point.

I want to be straight with you: four weeks is a block, not a lifetime transformation. The real size gains come from stacking blocks like this back to back, eating enough protein, sleeping properly, and showing up consistently. This programme gives you the training structure. The rest is on you.

### Who is this for, and who might want something else?

This is a good fit if you can commit to three gym sessions a week, you have access to a standard gym with barbells, dumbbells, cables, and basic machines, and you want a classic physique-focused split without overcomplicating things.

It might not be ideal if you can only train twice a week, you want pure powerlifting specificity, or you need a programme with no barbell work at all. Tempered Strength has other options for that, but this one is deliberately traditional.

It is free in the app, so you can run the full four-week block, log your sets, use the rest timers, and swap exercises where the programme allows it, without paying for a subscription. That makes it a low-risk way to try structured training and see if the app works for you.
