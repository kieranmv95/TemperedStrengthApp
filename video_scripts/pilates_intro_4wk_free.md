## Main body (~5 minutes)

### The big picture

Pilates Intro is built around three session formats that rotate through the week.

**Pilates A: Core and glutes.** Planks, glute bridges, side planks, controlled sit-ups, dead bugs. A control circuit with slow reps and steady breathing. A Tabata finisher for breath and core endurance: single-leg stretch and controlled leg lowers.

**Pilates B: Mobility and posture.** Cat-cow, thread the needle, hip flexor stretches, modified hundreds, leg circles, bird dogs, wall angels, and a breathing reset. This session opens the body and trains postural awareness.

**Pilates C: Endurance and flow.** Longer holds, flowing sequences, hollow holds, and movement patterns that link together. By week four you are moving with more confidence and less hesitation.

Each session starts with a matched warmup, runs through block-based circuits or timed work, and finishes with a cooldown tailored to what you just trained.

### How four weeks progress

**Week one: Learn the movements.** Shorter holds. More guidance in the notes. Every rep should feel deliberate. Knees-down planks and modified positions are encouraged. Form over duration, every time.

**Week two: Build endurance.** Holds get slightly longer. Rounds may increase. You know the positions now, so the focus shifts to maintaining quality for more time.

**Week three: Add complexity.** New movements appear. Flow sequences link exercises together. The control you built in weeks one and two pays off here.

**Week four: Integration.** Longer circuits, more demanding finishers, and sessions that feel like a complete Pilates practice rather than isolated exercises.

Intensity stays moderate throughout. This is not about grinding through pain. It is about control, breathing, and consistency.

### What to expect

Stronger core, better posture, improved mobility, and a tighter feel around your midsection. You will not add significant muscle mass or hit personal records on barbell lifts. You will move better, brace harder, and feel more stable in daily life and in the gym.

Many people run this alongside a strength programme as a core and mobility supplement. Twenty-five minutes three times a week is manageable even with a full lifting schedule.

### Who is this for?

Good fit if you want core and mobility work with structure, you are recovering from a period of inactivity, or you want a low-equipment option at home. Most exercises need only a mat. Some use bodyweight alone.

Less ideal if you want hypertrophy, powerlifting, or high-intensity conditioning. This is a different training stimulus entirely.

It is free in the app, which makes it an easy addition to test without commitment.
