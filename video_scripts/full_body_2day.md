## Intro (~60 seconds)

Not everyone can train five days a week. If you have a busy job, a family, or you simply do not want to live in the gym, but you still want to keep your strength and muscle, this programme is worth your attention.

The 2 Day Full Body programme in Tempered Strength is a twelve-week block. You train Saturday and Sunday, about sixty minutes per session. Two full body workouts, every week, for three months.

It is free, beginner-friendly, and built around maintenance with room for steady gains. The app describes it honestly: two sessions a week is enough to hold onto strength and muscle, protect your joints, and add small but steady progress across your back, legs, and chest.

Let me walk you through how it works, what changes across the twelve weeks, and whether two days is enough for what you are trying to achieve.

## Main body (~5 minutes)

### The big picture

This is not a minimalist programme that skips half your muscle groups. Both days are genuine full body sessions covering squat or leg press patterns, pressing, horizontal pulling, vertical pulling, and a conditioning or core finisher.

The twelve weeks split into three four-week cycles, each with a different emphasis.

**Cycle one: Foundation.** Building movement quality with barbell compounds. Barbell squat, barbell bench, barbell row, lat pulldown, and kettlebell swings on day A. Barbell deadlift, barbell overhead press, pull-ups, and planks on day B.

**Cycle two: Stability.** Emphasising machine-based stability and controlled range of motion. Leg press replaces squats on day A. Dumbbell shoulder press replaces barbell overhead press on day B. Hanging leg raises replace planks. The loads are controlled but the stimulus shifts toward stability and joint-friendly patterns.

**Cycle three: Hypertrophy.** Maximising muscle stimulus through dumbbell and free weight variation. Incline dumbbell press replaces barbell bench. T-bar row replaces barbell row. Single arm lat pulldown replaces standard lat pulldown. Cable crunches replace hanging leg raises. Rep ranges widen slightly on pressing work.

Intensity sits at seven for the first eight weeks and bumps to eight in the hypertrophy cycle, so the final month feels more demanding.

### Day A (Saturday)

Squat or leg press to open, depending on the cycle. Bench or incline dumbbell press. Horizontal row (barbell or T-bar). Vertical pull (lat pulldown or single arm variant). Kettlebell swings to finish: high reps, hip hinge focus, glute activation. Not AMRAP, because going to failure on swings risks form breakdown.

### Day B (Sunday)

Deadlifts first: five to eight reps, three-minute rest, the heaviest pull of the week. Overhead pressing (barbell or dumbbell). Pull-ups with swap options and AMRAP on the final set. A cycling core finisher: planks in foundation, hanging leg raises in stability, cable crunches in hypertrophy.

Day B is pulling and overhead pressing focused. Day A is squat, bench, and row focused. Together they cover the full body without redundant fatigue patterns.

### What to expect

You will not blow up in twelve weeks on two days a week. That is not the promise. The promise is that you keep what you have built, your joints stay healthy, your big lifts keep moving forward, and you add steady gains if nutrition and sleep support it.

This is ideal for someone returning to training after a break, someone maintaining during a busy period, or someone who supplements weekend gym sessions with other activity during the week.

### Who is this for?

Perfect if Saturday and Sunday are your gym days and you want structure rather than improvising. Less ideal if you want maximum hypertrophy, dedicated arm days, or daily training. For that, look at the 3 Day Split or the bodybuilding programmes.

You need a gym with barbells, dumbbells, cables, a leg press, and ideally a pull-up bar or lat pulldown. Pull-ups are swappable if needed.
